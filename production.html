Compositions

The Golden Ratio

The Golden Ratio is a way of filling a shot by starting with a tiny object and as an imaginary line spirals outwards, the objects grow larger. The Golden Ratio is a visual representation of the Fibonacci Sequence and is something many photographers and filmmakers have noticed in nature.

The Quadrant System

The quadrant system is a way of splitting the frame into fourths and only working in certain quadrants. As you can see in the still image on the left from Drive, only the top-left and the bottom-right quadrants are lit, and we naturally look at them anyway because they have a character in each. The Quadrant System may also be used to center an image in the middle of the frame.

The Rule of Thirds

This is probably one the most used composition in filmmaking and photography. You split the image into ninths and try to put your subject on one of the intersections of the lines. This puts the subject in one-third of the frame, and the other two-thirds show where the subject is. This framing becomes very noticeable in conversations between two people, but is also used in many other situations.

The 180° Rule

The 180° Rule refers to the 'rule' that while filming two characters or objects, you should film only on one side of them. You should make an imaginary line between the two items and stay on one side of it, unless you actually show the camera moving to the other side. This is to avoid confusion from the audience on where objects are located. Also note that the composition follows the Rule of Thirds.

Framing

There are many forms of framing that serve a lot of different purposes. For example, wide shots are most commonly used to show location, while closeups show characters' emotions and can build tension and suspense.
